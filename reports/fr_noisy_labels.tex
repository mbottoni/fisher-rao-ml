\begin{abstract}
Learning under noisy labels is a central challenge in modern machine learning.
While noise-tolerant losses such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Generalized
Cross Entropy (GCE) are well-understood theoretically, the behavior of
information-geometric divergences such as the Fisher--Rao (FR) distance has
not been systematically evaluated in this setting.
We conduct a controlled benchmark comparing six objectives across four datasets
spanning two architecture families: a two-layer MLP on UCI Digits, MNIST,
and FashionMNIST (flattened pixel inputs), and a four-layer ConvNet on
CIFAR-10 (10k-sample subset).
Our results reveal a striking \emph{architecture-dependent reversal}:
on MLP models, FR under-performs standard cross-entropy at moderate symmetric
noise rates (Digits sym\,40: $\Delta\mathrm{acc} = -3.2\%$, $p=0.002$,
0/10 wins); on the ConvNet, FR \emph{outperforms} KL at the same regime
(CIFAR-10 sym\,40: $+2.4\%$, 10/10 wins, $p=0.002$; sym\,60: $+3.4\%$, 10/10 wins, $p=0.002$).
By contrast, MAE---the theoretically optimal noise-tolerant loss---reverses
direction on ConvNets, falling $-9.6\%$ below KL at sym\,40 on CIFAR-10.
We trace the MLP failure to the Ghosh et al.\ noise-tolerance condition and
provide a formal analysis showing FR and Hellinger belong to the same family
that cannot satisfy it.
The ConvNet reversal suggests that optimization dynamics with batch
normalization and learned representations partially compensate for the missing
theoretical guarantee, and that MAE's flat gradients become detrimental in
this regime.
A gradient norm analysis (3-seed mean, sym\,40) reveals three distinct mechanisms:
KL memorises noisy samples (noisy/clean ratio $1.49\uparrow$), FR bounds gradients
(ratio $0.98\approx 1$), and GCE actively deweights (ratio $0.69\downarrow$),
explaining the observed performance hierarchy.
On MLP, FR's ratio grows to 2.61 by epoch 99 --- the same memorisation direction
as KL --- mechanistically explaining why gradient bounding requires ConvNet
structure to be beneficial.
A batch-normalization ablation (5 seeds) confirms FR is the most robust to BN removal
($-5.3\%$ mean drop vs.\ KL's $-13.3\%$).
Critically, FR and Hellinger also deliver dramatically better calibration under noise:
at sym\,40, FR achieves ECE\,$=0.025$ vs.\ KL's $0.233$ ($9\times$ improvement),
making them preferable not only for accuracy but for deployment reliability.
On CIFAR-N real human-annotated labels, FR is the only objective significantly
better than KL across all three noise conditions ($p=0.016$, 7/7 wins),
confirming the ConvNet advantage generalizes beyond the synthetic noise model.
These findings sharpen the understanding of where information-geometric losses are
and are not beneficial, and motivate further investigation of loss--architecture
interactions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Label noise is pervasive in large-scale datasets assembled from web crawls,
crowd-sourcing, and automated pipelines.
Even modest noise rates can substantially degrade test accuracy, and the
classical cross-entropy (KL) loss is known to be brittle in this setting.

Two families of remedies have been proposed.
\emph{Noise-tolerant losses} replace cross-entropy with objectives whose
gradients are inherently bounded or whose risk minimizers are provably
Bayes-optimal under symmetric noise~\citep{ghosh2017robust,
zhang2018generalized, wang2019symmetric}.
\emph{Robust training procedures} apply data selection, teacher--student
distillation, or sample weighting to reduce the influence of noisy
examples~\citep{han2018coteaching, li2020dividemix}.

A complementary line of work draws on \emph{information geometry}: replacing
KL divergence with the Fisher--Rao geodesic distance or the Hellinger distance
in the training objective.
These divergences are bounded, symmetric, and properly metric on the simplex,
properties that are absent from KL and that have been argued to confer
robustness~\citep{bottoni2026fisherrao}.
However, no systematic empirical study has compared FR/Hellinger against
established noise-tolerant baselines across a range of noise conditions.

We fill this gap with a controlled experiment whose design is simple enough to
be fully reproducible on a single laptop.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A six-way comparison of objectives (KL, Fisher--Rao, Hellinger, GCE,
    MAE, SCE) across noise regimes on four datasets spanning MLP and ConvNet
    architectures.
  \item The empirical observation that FR's behavior \emph{reverses} between
    architectures: harmful for MLP under symmetric noise, beneficial for
    ConvNet; MAE reverses in the opposite direction.
  \item A formal analysis consisting of two complementary results:
    (a) Theorem~\ref{thm:fr_no_ntc} shows FR, Hellinger, and any Bhattacharyya-form
    loss cannot satisfy the Ghosh noise-tolerance condition, explaining the MLP failure;
    (b) Proposition~\ref{prop:grad_saturation} shows FR's logit gradient saturates
    to zero as the model becomes confident in the true class, explaining the
    ConvNet recovery: once representations converge under BN, FR stops
    applying pressure toward corrupted labels.
  \item Mechanistic analysis via gradient norm trajectories (3-seed mean, sym\,40):
    KL memorises noisy samples (noisy/clean norm ratio 1.49$\uparrow$), FR bounds
    gradients (ratio 0.98$\approx$1), GCE actively deweights (ratio 0.69$\downarrow$),
    SCE deweights erratically (ratio 0.56, oscillating),
    explaining the empirical ordering GCE $>$ Hellinger $>$ FR $>$ SCE $>$ KL $>$ MAE at sym\,60.
    A parallel MLP analysis (Table~\ref{tab:mlp_grad_ratio}) shows that FR's gradient
    stability is architecture-specific: on MLP, FR's ratio grows to 2.61 (same
    direction as KL memorisation), mechanistically explaining why gradient bounding
    helps ConvNets but not MLPs.
    A batch-normalization ablation (5 seeds, all regimes) further shows FR is the
    most BN-robust objective ($-5.3\%$ mean drop vs.\ KL's $-13.3\%$).
  \item A ConvNet calibration result: FR achieves ECE\,=\,0.025 vs KL's 0.233
    at sym\,40 (a $9\times$ improvement), indicating that bounded-gradient objectives
    are preferable not only for accuracy but also for deployment reliability.
    The advantage is conditional on noise: at clean training, KL is significantly
    better calibrated than FR (ECE\,=\,0.044 vs 0.083, $p=0.002$).
  \item A CIFAR-N evaluation on real human-annotated labels showing that FR is
    the only objective that significantly outperforms KL at all three annotation
    conditions ($p=0.016$, 7/7 wins), with gains growing from $+0.7\%$
    (aggre) to $+1.6\%$ (worse, $\approx 40\%$ noise).
    MAE and SCE collapse at low-to-moderate noise ($p=0.016$); GCE
    significantly hurts at aggre ($p=0.031$), confirming the
    ConvNet reversal generalizes beyond the symmetric noise model.
  \item Practical guidance: for MLP/tabular data under symmetric noise use MAE
    or GCE; for ConvNet/image data under symmetric noise, GCE is strongest, FR and
    Hellinger reliable and well-calibrated; for ConvNet asymmetric noise, use
    Hellinger (same accuracy as KL, $3\times$ better ECE) or GCE
    — FR significantly hurts ($-2.7\%$, $p=0.002$).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Label noise types}

Let $(x, y) \sim \mathcal{D}$ be a clean example and $\tilde{y}$ the observed
noisy label.

\paragraph{Symmetric noise at rate $\varepsilon$.}
Each label is replaced uniformly at random by another class with probability
$\varepsilon$:
\[
  \tilde{y} \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    y & \text{with probability } 1-\varepsilon \\
    \mathrm{Uniform}(\mathcal{Y} \setminus \{y\}) & \text{with probability } \varepsilon.
  \end{cases}
\]

\paragraph{Asymmetric (class-conditional) noise at rate $\varepsilon$.}
Each class $c$ is mapped to class $(c+1)\!\mod C$ with probability $\varepsilon$
and remains $c$ otherwise.

\subsection{Objectives}

All objectives receive a one-hot target $\mathbf{y}$ (possibly corrupted) and
a predicted probability vector $\mathbf{p} = \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})$.

\paragraph{Cross-entropy (KL).}
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{KL} = -\sum_k y_k \log p_k$.

\paragraph{Fisher--Rao (FR).}
The geodesic distance on the categorical simplex, squared:
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{FR} = 4\arccos^2\!\bigl(\sum_k \sqrt{y_k p_k}\bigr)$.

\paragraph{Hellinger.}
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{H} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_k (\sqrt{y_k} - \sqrt{p_k})^2$.

\paragraph{Generalized Cross Entropy (GCE)~\citep{zhang2018generalized}.}
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{GCE} = (1 - p_{\tilde{y}}^q)/q$ with $q=0.7$.

\paragraph{Mean Absolute Error (MAE)~\citep{ghosh2017robust}.}
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{MAE} = \sum_k |y_k - p_k|$.

\paragraph{Symmetric Cross Entropy (SCE)~\citep{wang2019symmetric}.}
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{SCE} = 0.1\cdot\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{KL} +
1.0\cdot\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{RCE}$, where $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{RCE} = -\sum_k
p_k \log y_k$ is the reverse KL.

\subsection{Noise-tolerance condition}

\begin{proposition}[Ghosh et al.\ 2017, Theorem~1]
  \label{prop:ghosh}
  A loss $\ell(\mathbf{f}(x), \mathbf{e}_k)$ is Bayes-optimal under symmetric
  label noise with rate $\varepsilon < (C-1)/C$ if and only if
  $\sum_{k=1}^C \ell(\mathbf{f}(x), \mathbf{e}_k) = \mathrm{const}$
  for all $\mathbf{f}(x) \in \Delta^{C-1}$.
\end{proposition}

MAE satisfies this condition: $\sum_k \|\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{e}_k\|_1 = 2(C-1)$
for any $\mathbf{p}$~\citep{ghosh2017robust}.
Cross-entropy does not ($\sum_k -\log p_k$ is unbounded).

\begin{theorem}[FR and Hellinger do not satisfy the noise-tolerance condition]
\label{thm:fr_no_ntc}
Let $C \geq 3$ and let $f : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ be a strictly concave
function with $f(1) = 0$.
Define the class of \emph{Bhattacharyya losses}
\[
  \ell_f(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{e}_k) \;=\; g\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^C \sqrt{p_i}\,[\mathbf{e}_k]_i\right)
  \;=\; g(\sqrt{p_k}),
\]
where $g : [0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ is any strictly monotone function.
Then $\sum_{k=1}^C \ell_f(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{e}_k) = \sum_{k=1}^C g(\sqrt{p_k})$,
which is non-constant on $\Delta^{C-1}$ for any $g$ that is not
identically zero, so $\ell_f$ fails the Ghosh et al.\ condition.

In particular:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \emph{Fisher--Rao:} $\ell_\mathrm{FR}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{e}_k) = 4\arccos^2(\sqrt{p_k})$.
    Taking $g(t) = 4\arccos^2(t)$:
    \[
      \sum_{k=1}^C \ell_\mathrm{FR}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{e}_k) = 4\sum_{k=1}^C \arccos^2(\sqrt{p_k}).
    \]
    This is non-constant: for $\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{u} = (1/C,\ldots,1/C)$,
    $\sum_k = 4C\arccos^2(1/\sqrt{C})$, while for $\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{e}_1$,
    $\sum_k = 4(\pi/2)^2(C-1) = \pi^2(C-1)$.
    These differ for all $C \geq 2$.

  \item \emph{Hellinger:} $\ell_\mathrm{H}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{e}_k) =
    \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_i(\sqrt{p_i}-[\mathbf{e}_k]_i)^2 = 1 - \sqrt{p_k}$.
    Then $\sum_k \ell_\mathrm{H}(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{e}_k) = C - \sum_k\sqrt{p_k}$,
    which is non-constant since $\sum_k\sqrt{p_k}$ ranges from $1$ (at any $\mathbf{e}_k$)
    to $\sqrt{C}$ (at $\mathbf{u}$).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:fr_not_robust}
Under symmetric label noise with rate $\varepsilon \in (0,(C-1)/C)$, the
Fisher--Rao and Hellinger losses are \emph{not} guaranteed to be Bayes-optimal;
the empirical risk minimizer may converge to a solution worse than the Bayes
classifier.
\end{corollary}

The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:fr_no_ntc} follows directly from expanding the
sum and verifying non-constancy at the two extreme distributions $\mathbf{u}$
and $\mathbf{e}_1$.
Corollary~\ref{cor:fr_not_robust} follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:ghosh}
by contrapositive.

\begin{proposition}[Gradient Saturation under Confident Correct Predictions]
\label{prop:grad_saturation}
Let $\mathbf{p} = \sigma(\mathbf{z}) \in \Delta^{C-1}$ be the softmax prediction
and let $e_k$ be the assigned (possibly corrupted) label.
The gradient of the per-sample loss with respect to the logit $z_k$ satisfies:
\[
  \frac{\partial \ell_\mathrm{KL}}{\partial z_k} = p_k - 1 \longrightarrow -1
  \quad \text{as } p_k \to 0,
\]
\[
  \frac{\partial \ell_\mathrm{FR}}{\partial z_k}
    = -4\,\arccos(\sqrt{p_k})\cdot\sqrt{p_k}\cdot\sqrt{1-p_k}
    \longrightarrow 0
  \quad \text{as } p_k \to 0.
\]
Consequently, as the model becomes confident in the \emph{true} class
($p_{j^*} \to 1$, hence $p_k \to 0$ for a corrupted label $k \neq j^*$),
KL maintains near-maximal gradient pressure toward the wrong label,
while FR's gradient saturates to zero, preventing further memorisation.
\end{proposition}

The proof follows from direct differentiation: $\ell_\mathrm{FR} = 4\arccos^2(\sqrt{p_k})$,
so $\partial\ell_\mathrm{FR}/\partial p_k = -4\arccos(\sqrt{p_k})/(\sqrt{p_k}\sqrt{1-p_k})$,
and $\partial p_k/\partial z_k = p_k(1-p_k)$, giving the expression above.

\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:grad_saturation} provides a mechanistic complement to
Theorem~\ref{thm:fr_no_ntc}: while Theorem~1 shows FR is not
\emph{asymptotically} noise-tolerant, Proposition~\ref{prop:grad_saturation}
shows FR is \emph{dynamically} corruption-resistant once the model achieves
confident correct predictions on most samples.
This distinction explains the architecture-dependent reversal:
in ConvNets with batch normalisation, representations converge early enough
for gradient saturation to operate; in MLPs without such inductive structure,
corrupted gradients accumulate before saturation, and the Ghosh-condition
violation dominates.
\end{remark}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Datasets and architectures.}
We evaluate on four 10-class classification datasets spanning two architecture
families to isolate loss--architecture interactions.

\textit{MLP family (three datasets).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{UCI Digits}: 1,797 samples, 64 hand-crafted features.
    80/20 stratified train/test split; features standardized.
    10 seeds.
  \item \textbf{MNIST}: 5,000 training samples (stratified from 60k).
    28$\times$28 grayscale pixels flattened to 784 features.
    10 seeds.
  \item \textbf{FashionMNIST}: Same protocol as MNIST but Zalando fashion items.
    10 seeds.
\end{itemize}

\textit{ConvNet family (one dataset).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CIFAR-10}: 10,000 training and 2,000 test samples (stratified
    subsets).
    32$\times$32 RGB images; random crop and horizontal flip augmentation.
    10 seeds; noise regimes: clean, sym\,20\%/40\%/60\%, asym\,40\%
    (sym\,80\% excluded as impractical for ConvNets in this sample regime).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{MLP model.}
Two-hidden-layer MLP with 256 units per layer, batch normalisation, and ReLU
activations.

\paragraph{ConvNet model.}
Four-layer convolutional network: two Conv--BN--ReLU blocks with 64 channels
and MaxPool, two more blocks with 128 and 256 channels, followed by a
fully-connected head (512 units, BN, dropout).

\paragraph{MLP training.}
Adam optimiser, learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, cosine
annealing over 100 epochs, batch size 128, gradient clipping at 5.0.

\paragraph{ConvNet training.}
SGD with momentum 0.9, weight decay $5\times10^{-4}$; linear LR warm-up over
5 epochs to $10^{-2}$, then cosine annealing over 60 epochs; batch size 128.

\paragraph{Noise injection.}
Noise is applied to training labels only; the test set is always clean.
For symmetric noise at rate $\varepsilon$, we sample $\lfloor \varepsilon N
\rfloor$ training indices uniformly at random and replace their labels with a
uniformly random alternative class.
For asymmetric noise at rate $\varepsilon = 0.4$, each label $c$ is set to
$(c+1)\!\mod 10$ independently with probability $0.4$.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
Primary metric: test accuracy.
Secondary metrics: ECE (expected calibration error), Brier score, NLL.
Statistical comparison: two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test against KL with
paired seeds; oriented gain $= \mathrm{mean}(\Delta\mathrm{acc})$ with positive
meaning improvement over KL.
With $n=10$ seeds, the minimum achievable Wilcoxon $p$-value is $\approx 0.002$;
with $n=5$, it is $\approx 0.063$, so 5-seed cells report win counts and mean
gain only.

Key observations:

\paragraph{MLP family (Digits, MNIST, FashionMNIST).}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{MAE dominates at all symmetric noise levels.}
    On Digits at sym\,40, MAE achieves $89.6\%$ vs KL's $70.0\%$ ($+19.6\%$,
    $p=0.002$, 10/10 wins); on MNIST at sym\,40, MAE achieves $75.2\%$ vs
    KL's $61.6\%$ ($+13.6\%$, 10/10 wins).
  \item \textbf{FR and Hellinger are significantly worse than KL at sym\,40 and sym\,60.}
    Digits FR: $-3.2\%$ at sym\,40 ($p=0.002$, 0/10 wins);
    $-2.0\%$ at sym\,60 ($p=0.004$, 0/10 wins).
    MNIST FR: $-1.4\%$ at sym\,40 ($p=0.059$, 2/10 wins, directional);
    $-1.0\%$ at sym\,60 ($p=0.049$, 2/10 wins).
    FashionMNIST FR: $-1.5\%$ at sym\,40 ($p=0.004$, 1/10 wins);
    $-1.3\%$ at sym\,60 ($p=0.041$, 2/10 wins); both formally significant.
    Hellinger tracks FR closely on all three datasets, confirming the pattern is
    not dataset-specific; on FashionMNIST, Hellinger reaches significance at
    asym\,40 ($-1.3\%$, $p=0.002$, 0/10 wins) and sym\,80 ($-1.0\%$, $p=0.023$, 2/10 wins).
  \item \textbf{GCE consistently outperforms KL; SCE is unreliable.}
    On Digits, GCE/SCE achieve $+6.1$--$6.3\%$ at sym\,40.
    On MNIST, GCE achieves $+1.8\%$ ($p=0.004$) while SCE falls $-3.7\%$ ($p=0.004$).
  \item \textbf{At sym\,80, differences vanish.}
    All objectives converge near random ($\approx 19$--$29\%$) as SNR collapses.
  \item \textbf{Asymmetric noise: only MAE wins consistently.}
    Digits: $+6.2\%$ ($p=0.002$, 10/10 wins). MNIST: $+3.4\%$.
    FashionMNIST: $+7.6\%$ ($p=0.002$, 10/10 wins).
    On FashionMNIST at asym\,40, Hellinger ($p=0.002$, 0/10 wins) and SCE ($p=0.012$, 1/10 wins)
    significantly underperform KL.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{ConvNet family (CIFAR-10).}
The results in Table~\ref{tab:accuracy_cifar10} reveal a sharp reversal:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{FR and Hellinger now \emph{outperform} KL under symmetric noise, with strong statistical significance.}
    FR: $+2.4\%$ at sym\,20 (10/10 wins, $p=0.002$), $+2.4\%$ at sym\,40 (10/10 wins, $p=0.002$),
    $+3.4\%$ at sym\,60 (10/10 wins, $p=0.002$).
    Hellinger shows the same direction and is also significant at sym\,20--60 (all $p=0.002$, 10/10 wins).
    At high noise (sym\,60), Hellinger (+7.6\%) exceeds FR (+3.4\%), suggesting its
    more aggressive gradient clipping becomes more valuable as noise increases.
    SCE also significantly outperforms KL at sym\,60 (9/10 wins, $p=0.004$), but
    \emph{underperforms} KL at sym\,20 and sym\,40 (both $p=0.002$, 0/10 wins).
  \item \textbf{MAE severely underperforms on ConvNet.}
    At clean, MAE achieves $71.6\%$ vs KL's $84.1\%$ ($-12.5\%$, $p=0.002$, 0/10 wins).
    At sym\,40: $63.0\%$ vs $72.6\%$ ($-9.6\%$). This is the opposite direction from the MLP datasets.
  \item \textbf{GCE is the strongest overall on CIFAR-10 under high symmetric noise.}
    GCE achieves $+3.4\%$ at sym\,40 (10/10 wins, $p=0.002$) and $+9.5\%$ at sym\,60 (10/10 wins, $p=0.002$),
    the largest gain of any objective.
    The hierarchy at sym\,60 is: GCE $>$ Hellinger $>$ FR $>$ SCE $>$ KL $>$ MAE.
  \item \textbf{Asymmetric noise: FR hurts significantly; others neutral.}
    At asym\,40 (10 seeds), FR falls $-2.7\%$ (0/10 wins, $p=0.002$);
    Hellinger is neutral ($+0.1\%$, 7/10 wins, $p=0.625$); GCE is neutral ($-0.0\%$, $p=0.826$).
    MAE severely underperforms ($-12.7\%$, $p=0.002$).
\end{enumerate}

Table~\ref{tab:no_bn} reveals a striking \emph{differential sensitivity} to batch normalization
that is consistent across all noise levels and confirmed with 5 seeds.

\textbf{Fisher--Rao is the most robust.} FR drops only $2.1$--$7.1\%$ without BN —
smaller than KL's $10.6$--$16.6\%$ drop and far smaller than GCE ($27$--$38\%$),
Hellinger ($31$--$42\%$), and MAE ($31$--$49\%$).
SCE is the second-most robust objective (mean $-7.9\%$), consistent with its design as a
bounded symmetric divergence.
This ordering is preserved across every noise regime tested, strengthening the hypothesis.

\textbf{GCE and Hellinger collapse without BN.}
GCE's gradient scales as $p_k^{q-1}$; with $q=0.7 < 1$, gradient magnitude decreases
as confidence increases, starving the network once early layers adapt.
Hellinger's gradient satisfies $\partial \ell_H / \partial p_k \to 0$ as $p_k \to 1$
for the same reason.
Without BN's adaptive normalization, both objectives fail to provide useful signal in
the final training phase.

\textbf{MAE collapses worst at clean, improves slightly at high noise.}
MAE's flat gradient provides constant magnitude regardless of prediction correctness.
Without BN, the optimizer cannot exploit curvature and training largely fails at clean
regime ($-49\%$). Interestingly, MAE's drop shrinks at sym\,60 ($-33\%$): at very high
noise, prediction magnitudes are already noisy, reducing BN's effective contribution.

\textbf{Mechanistic interpretation.} FR's gradient magnitude is bounded by $\pi^2$
on any individual loss term, providing an intrinsic gradient clipping effect.
BN's adaptive standardization partially serves the same function: preventing gradient
explosion from high-confidence wrong predictions. When BN is removed, FR's bounded
gradient partially compensates, while KL, GCE, and Hellinger — which can produce
arbitrarily large gradients near simplex vertices — lose this stabilization entirely.

The 5-seed results confirm and sharpen the 2-seed qualitative pattern: FR and SCE are
structurally robust to BN removal, while GCE, Hellinger, and MAE collapse.

\textbf{Critical implication: BN is not the sole mediator.}
The BN-ablated results also reveal a counterintuitive pattern: \emph{FR beats KL even more
strongly without BN} than with it.
At sym\,40 without BN: FR\,=\,68.3\% vs KL\,=\,55.7\% ($+12.6\%$), compared to
$+2.4\%$ with BN.
This rules out the hypothesis that the ConvNet reversal is entirely mediated by BN:
FR's advantage persists and actually grows when BN is removed.
The ConvNet architecture itself (convolutional feature extraction and learned
representations) interacts with FR's gradient structure in a way that is
independent of batch normalization.

\section{Discussion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Why does FR fail under symmetric noise?}
The failure is explained by Proposition~\ref{prop:ghosh}: FR's per-class loss
values sum to a quantity that depends on the predicted distribution, so the
risk minimizer under symmetric noise is \emph{not} the Bayes classifier.
In contrast, MAE's constant-sum property makes it immune to symmetric noise by
construction.

The situation is conceptually similar to the KL vs.\ MAE comparison: KL also
lacks the constant-sum property and is therefore brittle to symmetric noise.
But KL at least benefits from numerical stability and well-understood
optimisation dynamics.
FR combines the worst of both worlds: it lacks the constant-sum property
\emph{and} its gradient vanishes near one-hot distributions (where
$\arccos(\sqrt{p_k}) \approx 0$), leaving the optimiser with weak signal
precisely when training examples are corrupted.

\paragraph{Why does FR reverse on ConvNets? Gradient norm analysis.}
The Ghosh et al.\ condition is an asymptotic, distributional statement about
risk minimizers.
In practice, with finite data and modern architectures, additional factors
intervene.
To isolate the mechanism, we measure the \emph{noisy/clean gradient norm ratio}
during training: the ratio of model gradient norm $\|\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}\|_2$
computed separately on a fixed held-out batch of mislabelled samples
vs.\ a fixed batch of correctly-labelled samples, each epoch
(experiment at sym\,40, 3-seed mean).

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Gradient norm ratio (noisy/clean) over training epochs (CIFAR-10,
    sym\,40, 3-seed mean). KL ratio increases monotonically (memorization);
    FR stabilises near 1.0 (equal treatment); GCE decreases (active downweighting).}
  \label{tab:grad_ratio}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lccccccc}
    \toprule
    Objective & Ep\,0 & Ep\,10 & Ep\,20 & Ep\,30 & Ep\,40 & Ep\,50 & Ep\,59 \\
    \midrule
    KL (CE)     & 1.04 & 1.24 & 1.24 & 1.35 & 1.41 & 1.53 & \textbf{1.49} $\uparrow$ \\
    Fisher--Rao & 0.85 & 0.71 & 0.74 & 0.80 & 0.91 & 0.98 & \textbf{0.98} $\approx 1$ \\
    Hellinger   & 0.81 & 0.65 & 0.60 & 0.66 & 0.79 & 0.84 & \textbf{0.88} $\approx 1$ \\
    GCE         & 0.74 & 0.50 & 0.51 & 0.55 & 0.57 & 0.68 & \textbf{0.69} $\downarrow$ \\
    MAE         & 0.68 & 0.55 & 0.66 & 0.55 & 0.65 & 0.49 & \textbf{0.65} erratic \\
    SCE         & 0.67 & 0.53 & 0.45 & 0.50 & 0.54 & 0.57 & \textbf{0.56} erratic \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Figure~\ref{fig:grad_ratio} plots the ratio trajectory for each objective.
Five qualitatively different gradient dynamics emerge:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{KL (memorisation, ratio $> 1$, increasing).}
    As the model grows confident in the correct class, $p_j \to 0$ on noisy samples
    and the logit gradient $\partial \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{KL}/\partial z_j = p_j - 1
    \to -1$ maintains near-maximal pressure toward the wrong label throughout training.
    By epoch 59, noisy samples drive 49\% larger gradient updates than clean ones —
    the hallmark of memorisation.

  \item \textbf{FR and Hellinger (gradient saturation, ratio $\approx 1$, stable).}
    By Proposition~\ref{prop:grad_saturation}, FR's logit gradient
    $\partial \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{FR}/\partial z_j =
    -4\arccos(\sqrt{p_j})\sqrt{p_j}\sqrt{1-p_j} \to 0$ as $p_j \to 0$:
    once the model correctly suppresses the corrupted label, FR stops applying
    pressure toward it.
    Hellinger's gradient $1 - 1/\sqrt{p_j}$ (via softmax chain rule) analogously saturates.
    Both ratios stabilise near 1.0: the model does not preferentially overfit to
    mislabelled samples, even late in training.
    Hellinger's slightly lower ratio (0.88 vs 0.98) is consistent with its earlier
    saturation, which may explain Hellinger's stronger result at sym\,60.

  \item \textbf{GCE (gradient warping, ratio $< 1$, decreasing).}
    GCE's gradient $\partial \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{GCE}/\partial p_j =
    -p_j^{q-1}$ \emph{decreases} as the model grows confident in the wrong class
    ($p_j \to 1$): the Box--Cox transform actively suppresses gradient signal on
    high-confidence wrong predictions.
    By epoch 59, GCE assigns 31\% \emph{less} gradient weight to noisy than to
    clean samples.
    This explains why GCE dominates at high noise (sym\,60): it is the only
    loss that explicitly deweights noisy examples dynamically during training.

  \item \textbf{MAE (flat gradients, ratio erratic).}
    MAE has constant loss magnitude $\sum_k |y_k - p_k| \in [0,2]$ irrespective
    of prediction confidence, leading to erratic noisy/clean ratios (0.49--0.68).
    This flat signal satisfies the Ghosh condition for MLP models but starves
    ConvNets of discriminative gradient needed to learn convolutional filters at
    moderate dataset scales.

  \item \textbf{SCE (hybrid deweighting, ratio $\approx 0.53$, erratic).}
    SCE combines $0.1 \cdot \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{KL}$ with
    $1.0 \cdot \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{RCE}$ (reverse cross entropy, treating
    $\hat{p}$ as distribution and $y$ as target).
    The RCE term is bounded and deweights confident wrong predictions, yielding a
    final ratio of 0.56 --- more aggressive than GCE --- but the superimposed KL
    component introduces instability: ratios oscillate between 0.45 and 0.67.
    This erratic trajectory explains SCE's mixed performance: beneficial on
    CIFAR-10 ConvNet (Table~\ref{tab:accuracy_cifar10}) but harmful on MNIST MLP
    ($-3.7\%$, $p=0.004$), where the instability is amplified.
\end{itemize}

This mechanistic picture explains the performance ordering at sym\,60:
GCE ($+9.5\%$, active deweighting) $>$ Hellinger ($+7.6\%$, tight bounding) $>$
FR ($+3.4\%$, bounding) $>$ SCE ($+1.8\%$, erratic hybrid) $>$ KL (memorisation) $>$ MAE (flat gradient failure).
It also explains the BN ablation ordering in Table~\ref{tab:no_bn}: FR's
intrinsic gradient bound partially substitutes for BN's implicit normalisation,
so FR is the most robust to BN removal (mean drop $-5.3\%$ vs KL $-13.3\%$).
GCE and Hellinger collapse without BN ($-32.9\%$ and $-37.5\%$) because their
gradient-warping and bounding properties depend on well-calibrated confidence
scores, which BN provides.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/gradient_norm_ratio.pdf}
  \caption{Noisy/clean gradient norm ratio over training epochs on CIFAR-10
    ConvNet (sym\,40, 3-seed mean). KL memorises (ratio $>1$, increasing); FR and Hellinger
    bound gradients (ratio $\approx$1, stable); GCE actively deweights noisy samples

The bounding and warping mechanisms are complementary:
we test whether combining FR (early-phase gradient bounding) with GCE
(late-phase active deweighting) as a two-phase curriculum outperforms either
alone in Appendix~\ref{sec:dynamic_loss}.
These are hypotheses supported by the data; additional controlled ablations
(varying dataset size, architecture depth) would be needed to isolate each factor
definitively.

The architecture-specific gradient dynamics now complete the mechanistic picture:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{ConvNet:} FR achieves ratio $\approx 1$ (BN + convolutional representations
    allow bounded gradients to provide true stability) $\to$ gradient benefit dominates $\to$ FR helps.
  \item \textbf{MLP:} FR's ratio grows to 2.61 (gradient stability lost) $\to$ the Ghosh
    condition violation dominates $\to$ FR hurts.
\end{itemize}
MAE is the only objective that maintains approximately stable ratios on \emph{both}
architectures (1.05 MLP, 0.65 ConvNet), consistent with its noise-tolerance guarantee.
However, on ConvNets its ratio of 0.65 ($<1$) produces a flat-gradient failure mode
that prevents feature learning, which is why MAE hurts accuracy on ConvNets.

\paragraph{When does FR help?}
Three regimes now have empirical support for FR outperforming KL:
(1) the soft-label setting~\citep{bottoni2026fisherrao}, where targets are
diffuse probability vectors rather than one-hot;
(2) ConvNet training on image data under moderate symmetric noise,
as shown in this work ($p=0.002$, 10/10 wins at sym\,20--60); and
(3) real human-annotated instance-dependent noise (CIFAR-N), where FR is
the only objective that significantly outperforms KL at all three annotation
conditions ($p=0.016$, 7/7 wins).
The gradient norm analysis (Tables~\ref{tab:grad_ratio}--\ref{tab:mlp_grad_ratio})
reveals the common thread: FR's bounded gradient maintains a stable
noisy/clean ratio ($\approx 1$) when the architecture (ConvNet with BN and
convolutional representations) supports it.
When this stability is absent (MLP: ratio grows to 2.61) or when asymmetric noise
requires fine-grained class discrimination, the Ghosh condition violation dominates
and FR hurts.

\paragraph{Practical recommendation.}
For MLP/tabular data under symmetric label noise: use MAE or GCE, which
satisfy the noise-tolerance condition or approximate it.
For ConvNet/image data under moderate symmetric noise: FR and GCE are
competitive; avoid MAE, whose flat gradients impair convolutional feature
learning at moderate dataset scales.
For MLP/tabular data under asymmetric noise: MAE is the clear winner
(consistent $+3$--$8\%$ gains across all three MLP datasets).
For ConvNet/image data under asymmetric noise: use Hellinger — it maintains
accuracy and achieves $3\times$ better ECE than KL ($0.034$ vs $0.101$)
while FR significantly hurts ($-2.7\%$, $p=0.002$).

\section{Related Work}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Noise-tolerant losses.}
The theoretical foundation for loss-function robustness to label noise was laid
by \citet{ghosh2017robust}, who proved that MAE satisfies the
\emph{noise-tolerance condition} (Proposition~\ref{prop:ghosh}) and is therefore
Bayes-optimal under symmetric noise.
\citet{zhang2018generalized} observed that MAE suffers from slow convergence
and proposed Generalized Cross Entropy (GCE), which continuously interpolates
between KL ($q\to 0$) and MAE ($q\to 1$) via the negative Box--Cox transform
$(1-p_k^q)/q$.
\citet{wang2019symmetric} introduced Symmetric Cross Entropy (SCE), combining
forward and reverse KL to address the ``zero-gradient'' problem of KL on
low-confidence predictions.
\citet{ma2020normalized} unified these approaches by showing that normalizing
any loss by the sum over classes restores the noise-tolerance condition;
Normalized Cross Entropy (NCE) and Normalized MAE (NMAE) follow as special
cases.
\citet{liu2020earlylearning} connected noise tolerance to early-learning
regularization, showing that KL memorizes corrupted labels progressively and
that periodic resetting can partially restore its noise resistance.

\paragraph{Sample-selection and co-learning methods.}
A complementary family of methods identifies and downweights noisy samples
rather than modifying the loss.
\citet{han2018coteaching} propose Co-Teaching, where two networks
simultaneously filter each other's noisy examples.
\citet{yu2019disagreement} refine this with the Update by Disagreement
strategy, keeping only samples where the two networks disagree.
\citet{li2020dividemix} combine GMM-based label noise estimation with
MixUp~\citep{zhang2018mixup} and semi-supervised learning to achieve
state-of-the-art results on CIFAR-N.
These methods are orthogonal to ours: they modify the training procedure
rather than the loss; combining them with FR is a natural direction for
future work.

\paragraph{Real-world noisy label benchmarks.}
\citet{wei2022learning} released CIFAR-N, which pairs CIFAR-10/100 images with
real human-annotated noisy labels from Amazon Mechanical Turk, enabling
controlled evaluation under realistic noise patterns.
\citet{xiao2015learning} introduced Clothing-1M, a large-scale dataset
(1~million images) where labels are generated from surrounding text and are
noisy by construction.
Both benchmarks are standard evaluation targets for noise-robust methods.
Section~\ref{sec:cifar_n} evaluates CIFAR-N; Clothing-1M remains future work.

\paragraph{Architecture and optimisation interactions.}
The sensitivity of noisy-label robustness to architecture has received
limited attention.
\citet{arpit2017memorization} showed that deep networks first learn
easy/clean examples and later memorize corrupted ones (the ``early
learning'' phenomenon), with depth and capacity influencing the rate.
\citet{jiang2018mentornet} use a teacher network trained on clean data to
reweight noisy examples, exploiting the training-order curriculum effect.
Our observation that FR \emph{helps} ConvNets but \emph{hurts} MLPs under
symmetric noise adds an explicit loss--architecture interaction to this
literature.

\paragraph{Information geometry and bounded divergences.}
Information-geometric divergences as loss functions for machine learning have
been studied in several contexts.
The $f$-divergence family (of which KL, Hellinger, and $\chi^2$ are members)
has been used for generative modeling~\citep{nowozin2016fgan} and
distributional robustness~\citep{duchi2021learning}.
The Fisher--Rao (Bhattacharyya) geodesic distance was applied to pattern
recognition by \citet{rao1945information} and later connected to statistical
manifold geometry by \citet{amari2016information}.
\citet{bottoni2026fisherrao} introduced FR as a training loss for
soft-label classification and dimensionality reduction, establishing
theoretical advantages over KL in those settings.
The present work is the first to evaluate FR specifically under the
symmetric/asymmetric label noise model of \citet{ghosh2017robust}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Limitations}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{itemize}
  \item The CIFAR-10 experiment uses only 10k training samples. Preliminary 50k
    results (3 seeds for clean, sym\,20, sym\,40; 2 seeds for sym\,60, asym\,40;
    all directional only, $p_{\min}=0.250$ for $n=3$) confirm the pattern:
    FR beats KL at sym\,20 ($+1.1\%$, 3/3) and sym\,40 ($+2.2\%$, 3/3);
    GCE also dominates at sym\,40 ($+2.2\%$, 3/3); MAE is near-neutral at
    sym\,40 ($+0.6\%$) but remains far below at sym\,60 ($-8.9\%$),
    consistent with the flat-gradient hypothesis.
    Scaling to ResNet-18 requires additional GPU compute.
  \item The CIFAR-10 experiment uses 10 seeds ($p_{\min}\approx0.002$, all regimes
    including asym\,40), and the BN ablation uses 5 seeds. These provide sufficient
    statistical power for all key comparisons.
  \item We evaluate only synthetic noise for the main CIFAR-10 results.
    The CIFAR-N section (Section~\ref{sec:cifar_n}) addresses real human-annotated
    noise; Clothing-1M and other large-scale real-world benchmarks remain future work.
  \item The gradient norm analysis (Table~\ref{tab:grad_ratio},
    Figure~\ref{fig:grad_ratio}) uses 3 seeds (CIFAR-10, sym\,40), providing
    stable estimates for all six objectives.
  \item The proposed explanations for the ConvNet reversal are hypotheses;
    controlled ablations (removing batch norm, varying dataset size, varying
    architecture depth) are needed to isolate causal factors.
  \item We do not evaluate co-learning, teacher--student, or semi-supervised
    approaches; the comparison is restricted to the loss function alone.
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

We conducted a systematic evaluation of the Fisher--Rao geodesic distance as a
training objective under label noise across four datasets spanning two
architecture families (MLP and ConvNet) and five to six noise regimes.

The central finding is an \emph{architecture-dependent reversal}: FR hurts MLP
models under symmetric noise ($-3.2\%$ on Digits at sym\,40, $p=0.002$) while
helping ConvNet models in the same regime ($+2.4\%$ on CIFAR-10 at sym\,40,
10/10 wins, $p=0.002$).
MAE reverses in the opposite direction: dominant on MLP ($+19.6\%$) but
severely harmful on ConvNet ($-9.6\%$, 0/10 wins, $p=0.002$).
This reversal is not predicted by the Ghosh et al.\ noise-tolerance condition,
which explains the MLP failure but not the ConvNet recovery.

With 10 seeds, the ConvNet results reveal a hierarchy under high symmetric noise
(sym\,60): GCE ($+9.5\%$) $>$ Hellinger ($+7.6\%$) $>$ FR ($+3.4\%$) $>$ SCE
($+1.8\%$) $>$ KL $>$ MAE ($-5.1\%$).
At asym\,40, FR significantly hurts ($-2.7\%$, $p=0.002$) while Hellinger and
GCE are neutral, suggesting FR's bounded gradient provides insufficient
discrimination for class-conditional noise.

A gradient norm analysis (3-seed mean, sym\,40) provides direct mechanistic evidence:
KL memorises noisy samples (noisy/clean ratio $1.49\uparrow$), FR and Hellinger
bound gradients (ratio $\approx 1$, stable), and GCE actively deweights
($0.69\downarrow$) — explaining the empirical ordering at sym\,60.
A parallel MLP analysis reveals that FR's gradient stability is architecture-specific:
on MLP, FR's ratio grows to 2.61 by epoch 99 (same memorisation direction as KL),
completing the mechanistic picture of the architecture reversal.
A 5-seed BN ablation confirms FR is the most BN-robust objective (mean drop
$-5.3\%$ vs.\ KL's $-13.3\%$), consistent with bounded gradient providing
implicit gradient clipping.

Beyond accuracy, FR and Hellinger deliver dramatically better calibration under
noisy training: at sym\,40, FR achieves ECE\,$=0.025$ vs.\ KL's $0.233$ ($9\times$
improvement), making them preferable not only for accuracy but for reliable uncertainty
quantification in deployment.

For MLP/tabular settings: MAE and GCE are the principled choices for symmetric noise;
for asymmetric noise, only MAE wins consistently across all three MLP datasets.
For ConvNet/image settings under symmetric noise: GCE is the strongest performer;
FR and Hellinger are reliable alternatives with the additional benefit of excellent
calibration.
For ConvNet asymmetric noise: Hellinger maintains accuracy and provides substantially
better calibration (ECE\,=\,0.034 vs.\ KL's 0.101; Brier score $p=0.002$, 10/10 wins);
FR significantly hurts accuracy ($-2.7\%$, $p=0.002$).
Under real instance-dependent noise (CIFAR-N): FR and Hellinger are the best choices;
GCE can hurt at low noise levels and MAE collapses entirely.

On CIFAR-N real human-annotated noisy labels, FR is the only objective that
significantly outperforms KL at all three annotation conditions ($p=0.016$,
7 seeds): aggre $+0.7\%$, random1 $+1.0\%$, worse $+1.6\%$ ($\approx 40\%$ noise).
MAE and SCE significantly hurt at low-to-moderate noise ($p=0.016$); GCE
significantly hurts at aggre ($p=0.031$).
These formally significant results confirm the ConvNet advantage holds under
instance-dependent noise, independent of the synthetic noise model.

Future work should (i) scale to ResNet-18 on full CIFAR-10 to confirm the
finding at the 5$\times$ larger dataset and deeper architecture scale,
(ii) investigate the two-phase curriculum combining FR's early gradient stability
with GCE's late-phase denoising (Appendix~\ref{sec:dynamic_loss}),
and (iii) develop a theoretical framework
accounting for architecture and optimization dynamics beyond the asymptotic
Ghosh et al.\ analysis.

\section{Dynamic Loss-Switching Curriculum}
\label{sec:dynamic_loss}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
The gradient norm analysis (Table~\ref{tab:grad_ratio}) reveals three complementary
mechanisms: KL memorises, FR bounds, GCE actively deweights.
Since FR stabilises the noisy/clean gradient ratio early in training
(preventing premature memorisation) while GCE increasingly downweights noisy
samples late in training (actively denoising), a two-phase curriculum is a
natural test of whether these mechanisms are compositional:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Phase 1 (epochs 0--29):} Train with FR to establish stable
    gradient dynamics and learn discriminative features without memorising
    corrupted labels.
  \item \textbf{Phase 2 (epochs 30--59):} Switch to GCE to actively suppress
    high-confidence wrong predictions and refine the decision boundary.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
Same 4-layer ConvNet, 10k CIFAR-10 subset, 5 seeds, 5 noise regimes as the main
experiment (Section~\ref{sec:setup}).
We compare 5 two-phase schedules (\texttt{FR$\to$GCE}, \texttt{FR$\to$KL},
\texttt{GCE$\to$FR}, \texttt{KL$\to$GCE}, \texttt{FR$\to$MAE}) against
all 6 static-objective baselines (KL, FR, GCE, MAE, Hellinger, SCE),
with switch epoch fixed at 30.
