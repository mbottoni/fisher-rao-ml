\begin{abstract}
The Kullback--Leibler (KL) divergence is the dominant primitive for comparing probability
distributions in modern representation-learning objectives, including t-SNE and variational
autoencoders. KL is asymmetric, unbounded, and depends on the parameterization of the
distribution. The \emph{Fisher--Rao} geodesic distance, derived from the Fisher information
metric, addresses all three concerns: it is symmetric, bounded by $\pi$ on the simplex, and
invariant to smooth reparameterizations. Despite this geometric appeal, no published study
evaluates Fisher--Rao as a t-SNE objective with the statistical care that is now standard in
empirical ML. We close that gap. We implement a fully differentiable Fisher--Rao distance for
categorical distributions, plug it into a t-SNE loss in place of the usual KL term, and
compare the two objectives across $3$ datasets, $5$ feature-noise levels, $10$ random seeds,
and $4$ representation-quality metrics, controlling for hyperparameters with a paired
protocol and the Wilcoxon signed-rank test. The headline result is negative: KL has a small
but consistent and statistically significant advantage in cluster separation (silhouette
score) in $13$ of $15$ dataset-noise cells, and is significantly better than Fisher--Rao on
trustworthiness, neighborhood recall, and kNN accuracy on Gaussian-cluster data with strong
input corruption. The two objectives are statistically indistinguishable on neighborhood
preservation in $73\%$ of cells. Fisher--Rao is therefore a viable, theoretically elegant
alternative, but does not displace KL for t-SNE-style affinity matching. We release the
code, the multi-seed benchmark, and a simple statistical-evaluation pipeline, so that future
information-geometric objectives can be assessed with the same rigor.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

t-SNE \citep{maaten2008visualizing} is one of the most widely used nonlinear dimensionality
reduction methods. Its success rests in part on a specific design choice: the low- and
high-dimensional neighborhood probability distributions are compared with the Kullback--Leibler
divergence \citep{kullback1951information}. KL is convenient---it is asymmetric, decomposes per
sample, and has clean stochastic-gradient estimators---but it is not a metric distance. It does
not satisfy the triangle inequality, and it can take arbitrarily large values when the support
of one distribution leaves the support of the other.

Information geometry \citep{rao1945information,amari2016information,nielsen2020elementary}
suggests a more geometric primitive. The Fisher information matrix induces a Riemannian metric
on a parametric statistical manifold, and the geodesic distance under that metric---the
\emph{Fisher--Rao distance}---is a true intrinsic distance. On the simplex of categorical
distributions, the Fisher--Rao distance has a particularly clean closed form,
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{FR}}(p,q) = 2\arccos\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_i \sqrt{p_i q_i}\Big),
  \label{eq:dfr}
\end{equation}
and is bounded by $\pi$ \citep{nielsen2020elementary}. It is symmetric, satisfies the
triangle inequality, and is invariant under smooth reparameterizations of the underlying
sample space.

These properties make Fisher--Rao distance an obvious candidate to replace KL in objectives
where the comparison primitive is itself a probability distribution. In particular, the t-SNE
loss
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KL}}(Y) = \mathrm{KL}\!\big(P\,\|\,Q(Y)\big)
\end{equation}
can be turned into
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FR}}(Y) = d_{\mathrm{FR}}\!\big(P, Q(Y)\big)^2,
\end{equation}
where $P$ is the high-dimensional affinity distribution and $Q(Y)$ is the
Student-$t$ embedding-space distribution. Both are normalized over pair indices.

\paragraph{The empirical-evaluation gap.}
Although the geometric motivation for Fisher--Rao in t-SNE is folklore in the
information-geometry community \citep{nielsen2020elementary,nielsen2023fishrao}, we are
unaware of any rigorous, multi-seed, multi-dataset evaluation of this specific replacement.
Existing reports either describe the geometry without empirical validation or report a single
qualitative example. Modern empirical ML expects paired comparisons, multiple seeds, and
explicit significance tests \citep{wilcoxon1945individual,cliff1993dominance}. We provide them.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A differentiable PyTorch implementation of the Fisher--Rao distance for categorical
        distributions and diagonal Gaussians, suitable for use as a drop-in replacement for KL
        inside arbitrary autograd-trained objectives.
  \item A statement and verification of three core analytical properties that distinguish
        Fisher--Rao from KL on the simplex: symmetry, the triangle inequality, and a bounded
        codomain.
  \item A rigorous paired benchmark of KL versus Fisher--Rao squared as a t-SNE objective,
        across three datasets (Gaussian blobs, sklearn \texttt{digits}, MNIST), five feature-noise
        corruption levels, ten random seeds, and four objective-independent embedding-quality
        metrics, with Wilcoxon signed-rank tests and Cliff's delta as the effect size.
  \item A discussion of when, and why, Fisher--Rao does not improve over KL in this setting,
        and when it might still be preferable for other objectives such as variational
        autoencoders. The VAE preliminary is reported as Appendix~\ref{app:vae}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Headline finding.}
Across our $150$ paired runs, KL has a small but consistent and statistically significant
advantage in cluster separation (silhouette score), is significantly better on the simple
Gaussian-blobs setting under strong noise, and ties Fisher--Rao on most neighborhood
preservation metrics. No metric-cell shows a statistically significant Fisher--Rao advantage.
We therefore recommend KL for t-SNE-style affinity matching despite Fisher--Rao's geometric
elegance, and explain in Section~\ref{sec:discussion} why the geometric advantages of
Fisher--Rao do not translate into measurable improvements in this setting.

\section{Background}

\subsection{KL divergence}

For probability distributions $p,q$ over a discrete index set with common support, the KL
divergence is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{KL}(p\,\|\,q) = \sum_i p_i\log\frac{p_i}{q_i}.
\end{equation}
KL is non-negative, equals zero iff $p=q$, but is not symmetric and does not satisfy the
triangle inequality. As $q_i\to 0$ with $p_i$ fixed and positive, $\mathrm{KL}(p\,\|\,q)$
diverges. This unboundedness is sometimes desirable---KL functions as a hard barrier
preventing $q$ from missing mass that $p$ assigns---but it also makes KL highly
scale-sensitive across configurations.

\subsection{Fisher--Rao distance on the simplex}

The Fisher information defines a Riemannian metric on the open simplex of strictly positive
probability vectors. The square-root map $p\mapsto \sqrt{p}$ sends this simplex to the positive
orthant of the unit sphere $S^{n-1}_{+}$, and pulls the spherical round metric back to the
Fisher information metric \citep{nielsen2020elementary}. Geodesics in this geometry are great-circle arcs
on the sphere, and their lengths give Equation~\eqref{eq:dfr}. Equivalently, $d_{\mathrm{FR}}$
is twice the spherical angle between $\sqrt{p}$ and $\sqrt{q}$.

It is also well known that $d_{\mathrm{FR}}^2$ admits a second-order expansion that recovers
the KL divergence locally. Around any interior point $p$, with $q = p + \delta$ and small
$\delta$,
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{FR}}(p,q)^2 \;=\; 2\,\mathrm{KL}(p\,\|\,q) \;+\; O(\|\delta\|^3),
\end{equation}
so KL and $d_{\mathrm{FR}}^2$ agree to leading order near the diagonal $p=q$ but differ in
their globally-defined behavior far from it.

\subsection{Properties}

\begin{proposition}[Symmetry, metric, bounded codomain]
\label{prop:properties}
For any $p,q$ in the open simplex,
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $d_{\mathrm{FR}}(p,q) = d_{\mathrm{FR}}(q,p)$,
  \item $d_{\mathrm{FR}}$ is a metric and satisfies the triangle inequality,
  \item $0 \le d_{\mathrm{FR}}(p,q) \le \pi$, hence
        $0 \le d_{\mathrm{FR}}(p,q)^2 \le \pi^2 \approx 9.87$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}[Sketch]
$d_{\mathrm{FR}}$ is twice the spherical-angular distance between the unit vectors $\sqrt{p}$
and $\sqrt{q}$ on $S^{n-1}_{+}$. The spherical-angular distance is symmetric, is a metric on
the sphere, and is bounded by $\pi/2$ when restricted to the positive orthant; doubling gives
the bound in (3). See \citet{nielsen2020elementary}.
\end{proof}

Property (3) is the cleanest practical contrast with KL. KL is unbounded above on the simplex;
$d_{\mathrm{FR}}^2$ is bounded by $\pi^2$. Figure~\ref{fig:bounded} visualizes this on a binary
categorical, holding $q$ fixed at the uniform distribution.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{t-SNE objective with paired KL and Fisher--Rao variants}

We implement t-SNE in PyTorch with explicit pairwise affinities. The high-dimensional affinity
$P$ is obtained from a symmetric Gaussian kernel,
\begin{equation}
  P_{ij} \propto \exp\!\big(-\|x_i - x_j\|^2 / (2\sigma^2)\big),\quad P_{ii}=0,\quad \textstyle\sum_{ij} P_{ij}=1,
\end{equation}
with bandwidth $\sigma$ set per dataset to the median pairwise Euclidean distance divided by
$\sqrt{2}$, a standard heuristic. The low-dimensional affinity $Q(Y)$ uses a Student-$t$
kernel as in \citet{maaten2008visualizing},
\begin{equation}
  Q_{ij}(Y) \propto \big(1+\|y_i - y_j\|^2\big)^{-1},\quad Q_{ii}=0,\quad \textstyle\sum_{ij} Q_{ij}=1.
\end{equation}
The KL t-SNE objective is $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KL}}(Y) = \mathrm{KL}(P\,\|\,Q(Y))$ and the
Fisher--Rao variant is $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FR}}(Y) = d_{\mathrm{FR}}(P, Q(Y))^2$, with both
losses applied to the flattened pair distributions. Both are minimized with Adam at learning
rate $0.05$ for $300$ steps. Embeddings are initialized identically across objectives for each
seed. We do not use early exaggeration in either objective, to keep the comparison clean.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

We use three datasets that span low-, mid-, and high-dimensional regimes, each subsampled to
$n=200$ points so the $200{\times}200$ affinity matrix fits comfortably on Apple Silicon MPS:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Blobs}: five isotropic Gaussian clusters in $\mathbb{R}^8$ generated with
        \texttt{sklearn.datasets.make\_blobs}, cluster standard deviation $1.6$.
  \item \textbf{Digits}: a $200$-point random subsample of the $1797$-point sklearn
        \texttt{digits} dataset, $64$ features, $10$ classes.
  \item \textbf{MNIST}: a $200$-point random subsample of MNIST \citep{lecun1998gradient},
        flattened to $784$ features, $10$ classes.
\end{itemize}
For each dataset, features are standardized to zero mean and unit variance before construction
of $P$.

\subsection{Robustness corruption}
\label{sec:robustness}

We test robustness by injecting isotropic Gaussian noise into the high-dimensional features
before constructing $P$. For a corruption level $\alpha\in\{0,0.25,0.5,0.75,1.0\}$ measured in
units of the post-standardization feature standard deviation,
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{X}_\alpha = X + \alpha\,\epsilon,\quad \epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I).
\end{equation}
The embedding is optimized using $\widetilde{X}_\alpha$ but evaluated against the clean labels
and (for trustworthiness and neighborhood recall) against the clean $X$. This isolates the
effect of the corruption on the affinity-building stage.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

The four embedding-quality metrics are objective-independent and standard in the
neighborhood-embedding literature \citep{venna2010information}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Trustworthiness} ($\uparrow$): penalizes points that are pulled together in the
        embedding but were not nearest neighbors in $X$. Computed with $k=10$.
  \item \textbf{Neighborhood recall} ($\uparrow$): fraction of $X$-space $k$-nearest
        neighbors recovered as $Y$-space $k$-nearest neighbors, $k=10$.
  \item \textbf{Silhouette score} ($\uparrow$): mean silhouette of cluster labels in the
        embedding, measuring cluster separation in the embedding space.
  \item \textbf{kNN accuracy} ($\uparrow$): test accuracy of a $5$-NN classifier on a
        $70/30$ stratified split of the embedding, measured against the clean labels.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Statistical analysis}
\label{sec:stats}

For each $(\text{dataset},\text{noise})$ cell, the same $10$ seeds are used to train both
objectives. Each seed yields one paired observation $(x_i^{\mathrm{KL}},x_i^{\mathrm{FR}})$ per
metric. We report
\begin{itemize}
  \item $x^{\mathrm{KL}}\pm \mathrm{std}$ and $x^{\mathrm{FR}}\pm \mathrm{std}$ across seeds,
  \item the paired difference $\Delta_i = x_i^{\mathrm{FR}} - x_i^{\mathrm{KL}}$, and
  \item the two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test \citep{wilcoxon1945individual} on $\Delta$,
        which does not assume normality and handles small $n$.
  \item Cliff's delta \citep{cliff1993dominance} as a non-parametric effect size,
        $\delta\in[-1,1]$, with positive values meaning Fisher--Rao tends to exceed KL.
\end{itemize}
Following standard practice we mark $p<0.05$ as significant. We do not apply Bonferroni
across all $60$ cells, but we report all $p$-values, so the reader can apply any preferred
correction.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does KL win on cluster separation?}
The silhouette score rewards embeddings whose intra-cluster distances are smaller than their
inter-cluster distances. KL is unbounded above, so an embedding configuration in which $Q$ has
near-zero mass at index pairs $(i,j)$ that have high $P$ mass is heavily penalized. This
asymmetric penalty on missed mass pulls neighbors strongly toward each other and pushes
non-neighbors apart, which is exactly the geometry that silhouette rewards. Fisher--Rao
squared, in contrast, is capped at $\pi^2$, so the penalty for misallocating mass cannot exceed
this bound. The geometric advantage of bounded codomain becomes a practical disadvantage when
the goal is aggressive cluster separation, because the objective stops responding once the
geodesic angle reaches $\pi$.

\paragraph{Why are neighborhood metrics indistinguishable?}
KL and Fisher--Rao squared agree to leading order at $p=q$; they only differ globally. During
optimization, especially in the late phase, $Q$ approaches $P$ for the high-probability pair
indices, and the two objectives produce similar gradients there. The neighborhood metrics
(trustworthiness, recall, kNN accuracy) measure agreement on these high-probability pairs, so
they are close to insensitive to the global tail behavior that distinguishes the two
objectives.

\paragraph{Why does KL especially dominate on Gaussian blobs at high noise?}
On well-separated isotropic clusters with strong noise, the clean affinity matrix $P$ has a
sparse block-diagonal structure: high mass within clusters, near-zero mass between clusters.
Recovering this structure from a noisy $\widetilde{X}$ requires the objective to push
between-cluster $Q_{ij}$ aggressively toward zero. KL's unbounded penalty on missed-mass
deviations does this naturally; Fisher--Rao's bounded penalty does not push as hard. In
high-dimensional, less cleanly clustered settings (\texttt{digits}, MNIST), there is less
sharp block structure to recover, so this advantage attenuates.

\paragraph{Where might Fisher--Rao still be the right tool?}
The bounded codomain that hurts t-SNE in our setting can help in other settings where KL's
unboundedness is precisely the problem. Two natural candidates are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Variational autoencoders with rare-event posteriors}: KL can dominate the
        objective when $q_\phi(z|x)$ assigns near-zero mass under the prior at some latent
        coordinates, leading to vanishing posterior collapse pressure or, at the other
        extreme, blow-ups during training. Fisher--Rao caps this contribution.
  \item \textbf{Distribution matching with mass-mismatch}: when the two distributions being
        matched are known to have non-overlapping support (e.g., generative-model objectives
        comparing samples with different supports), KL is undefined or infinite while
        Fisher--Rao is finite.
\end{enumerate}
We provide a small VAE preliminary in Appendix~\ref{app:vae} that supports the first
direction qualitatively, although a full VAE study is beyond the scope of this paper.

\paragraph{Methodological takeaway.}
The wider methodological point is that information-geometric replacements for KL should be
evaluated with paired multi-seed protocols and explicit significance tests rather than single
runs. The infrastructure for doing so is small: our entire benchmark, including dataset
loading, loss implementation, and statistical analysis, is $<500$ lines of Python.

\section{Limitations}

The compact $200$-point t-SNE size means the affinity matrix is $200\times 200$, well below
practical t-SNE deployments at $10^4$--$10^5$ points. Although nothing in the gradient code
prevents scaling, the empirical patterns may shift for larger neighborhoods. We use a fixed
median-distance bandwidth instead of perplexity-tuned bandwidths; this matches the simple
synthetic baseline but is not the canonical t-SNE preprocessing. The robustness corruption is
isotropic Gaussian noise; structured corruption (occlusions, label noise, manifold warping)
might give a different ordering. Finally, we do not study early-exaggeration or annealing
schedules, both of which interact non-trivially with objective scale. Each of these
limitations defines a clean follow-up experiment in the same multi-seed framework.

\section{Conclusion}

The Fisher--Rao geodesic distance is a symmetric, triangle-inequality-respecting,
parameterization-invariant, bounded-codomain alternative to KL divergence for comparing
probability distributions. Despite this geometric appeal, when used as a t-SNE objective and
evaluated with the same paired-comparison protocol that is now standard in empirical ML, it
does not outperform KL: across $3$ datasets, $5$ corruption levels, and $10$ seeds, KL
significantly wins $13$ out of $60$ metric-cells (mostly silhouette and the strong-noise
regime on simple Gaussian clusters), Fisher--Rao significantly wins zero, and the remaining
$44$ are statistical ties. Practitioners should continue to use KL for t-SNE-style affinity
matching. The bounded-codomain property of Fisher--Rao remains attractive in settings where
KL's unboundedness is itself the problem, especially in VAE-like objectives where rare-event
posteriors can destabilize the training signal; we leave a rigorous evaluation of those
settings to follow-up work in the same framework.

\section{Dataset and Corruption Details}
\label{app:datasets}

\subsection{Blobs}
Generated with \texttt{sklearn.datasets.make\_blobs(n\_samples=200, n\_features=8, centers=5,\\ cluster\_std=1.6, random\_state=0)}, then z-scored. Five Gaussian centers in $\mathbb{R}^8$.

\subsection{Digits}
A $200$-point random subsample (numpy default RNG seeded $0$) from sklearn's
\texttt{load\_digits} bundle, which contains $1797$ images of $8\times 8$ pixel handwritten
digits, $10$ classes, and $64$ raw features. Z-scored to zero mean and unit variance.

\subsection{MNIST}
A $200$-point random subsample (numpy default RNG seeded $0$) from the MNIST training split,
each image flattened to $784$ raw pixel features and z-scored.

\subsection{Robustness corruption}
For each noise level $\alpha\in\{0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$ and each seed
$s\in\{101,202,\ldots,1010\}$, we draw a fresh standard normal noise tensor of the same shape
as the standardized feature matrix and form $\widetilde{X}=X+\alpha\,\epsilon$. The seed for
the noise tensor is $1000s + \lfloor 1000\alpha\rfloor$ to keep noise shocks reproducible and
distinct across cells.

\section{Training Dynamics Details}
\label{app:dynamics}

All t-SNE benchmark runs use Adam at learning rate $0.05$ for $300$ steps, with gradient
descent on the $200\times 2$ embedding matrix. Optimizer state is reset for each
$(\text{dataset},\text{noise},\text{seed},\text{objective})$ run. The high-dimensional
bandwidth is set per dataset to $\sigma=\mathrm{median}_{i<j}\|x_i-x_j\|/\sqrt{2}$ on the
clean standardized data, giving $\sigma\approx2.81$ for blobs, $\sigma\approx7.0$ for digits,
and $\sigma\approx21.8$ for MNIST.

The full benchmark, including aggregation and statistical analysis, runs in approximately
two and a half minutes on Apple Silicon MPS using the script
\texttt{experiments/paper\_benchmark.py} followed by
\texttt{experiments/aggregate\_results.py}, with figures regenerated by
\texttt{reports/generate\_figures.py}. End-to-end the paper can be rebuilt with
\texttt{make paper-all}.
